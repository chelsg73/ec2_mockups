Important things about Early Check

Early Check is a research study.

  • When you sign up your baby for Early Check, you are joining a research study. The goal of Early Check is to find serious health conditions in babies so they can get help sooner.

Early Check testing is free and painless.

  • There are no charges to you or your insurance plan for taking part in Early Check. The costs of the screening test and information about the results are covered by the study.
  • The tests don’t require any extra blood to be drawn from your baby. They use blood that is already taken through a regular heel prick after birth.
  • Click [arrow] below to see costs Early Check covers.

    Costs covered by Early Check include:

    • Early Check screening tests,
    • help in understanding what results mean,
    • collection of a spit (saliva) sample (if needed),
    • and a second test for any baby whose screening test is not normal.

    If a second test confirms the health problem, Early Check will provide for free:

    • genetic counseling and education about the health problem,
    • an assessment of the baby’s development, and
    • help finding doctors and support services.
  • If your baby receives a not normal result from a screening test, additional testing or examinations may be needed. Early Check may not pay for this additional testing. Early Check also does not pay for doctor and specialist office visits or treatments for your baby.

Early Check is not a substitute for regular newborn screening.

  • Early Check is not the same as standard North Carolina newborn screening. Learn more about North Carolina newborn screening below.

    North Carolina newborn screening is a free program.

    In the hospital or birthing center, a nurse or other healthcare professional will prick the baby’s heel with a small needle to collect a few drops of blood. The hospital sends the drops of blood to the North Carolina State Laboratory of Public Health (NCSLPH) [add link] in Raleigh for testing. Basic information about the baby, such as date of birth, sex, and birth weight, is also sent.

    This is the same blood sample that will be used for Early Check screening tests. Early Check also uses this basic information about the baby to make sure the screening result is as accurate as possible.

  • If you choose not to join Early Check, your baby will still have regular North Carolina newborn screening and follow up.
  • Click here to see the differences between regular North Carolina newborn screening and Early Check.

    This table shows the differences between regular North Carolina newborn screening and Early Check.

    Early Check Regular Newborn Screening
    Parents must give permission for their babies to be screened Yes No
    There are treatments for babies with the health problems that can greatly improve their symptoms Maybe* Yes
    Type of Program Research Public Health

    *There are no cures for these health problems but there are things that may help. Researchers are looking for better treatments.

Screening tests are not perfect.

  • Early Check screening will not find all health conditions in all babies.
  • If Early Check finds a potential health condition in a baby, follow-up testing is done to learn more.
  • All babies are different. For babies with the health conditions, Early Check and doctors will not know exactly when their symptoms will start and how severe the symptoms will be.

Joining Early Check is voluntary. You can withdraw at any time.

  • If you’d like to withdraw, contact us at any time. If the baby’s Early Check screening test has already been done, we’ll still contact you if the screening test is not normal.
